\section{Determination of suns surface temperature with an
experiment}\label{determination-of-suns-surface-temperature-with-an-experiment}}

We try to investigate the temperature of the sun's surface by measuring
the temperature change of a water filled and blacked test tube when
exposed to the sun for a certain time.

\hypertarget{mathematical-background}{%
\subsubsection{Mathematical background}\label{mathematical-background}}

To determine the suns surface temperature in this experiment we need
four relations. First, we will use the Stefan--Boltzmann-law

\[P=e\sigma AT^4\]

Here the power \(P\) is defined by the emissivity \(e\) (equal to 1 for
the sun since it is an almost perfect black body), the Stefan--Boltzmann
constant \(\sigma = 5.670373 \times 10^{-8} Wm^{-2}K^{-4}\) and the
temperature \(T\) in Kelvin.

Second is the intensity of radiation \(I\) defined as

\[I=\frac{P}{A}\]

with the power \(P\) (here the sun) and the illuminated or radiating
area \(A\). For the latter we have to compare the area of the sun's
surface to the area occupied by a sphere with the radius of one AU -
Astronomical Unit or mean distance between earth and sun. Since the
distance is far greater and the same power is radiated into this much
larger area, the intensity is much lower.

To measure the irridated and absorbed power \(P\) here on earth we
measure the change in internal energy \(Q\) over time \(t\)

\[P=\frac{Q}{t}\]

Last we have to determinte the change in internal energy \(Q\). This
follows the relation

\[Q=mc\Delta T\]

with the mass \(m\) of the heated water, the specific heat capacity
\(c=4.184 Jg^{-1}K^{-1}\) of water and the temperature change
\(\Delta T\).

\hypertarget{setup}{%
\subsubsection{Setup}\label{setup}}

We placed a test tube with \(22 mm\) diameter into the sun. Filled with
\(30 ml\) water the filled height was \(100 mm\). The illuminated area
can therefore be estimated as a rectangular with the length of
\(100 mm\) and width of \(22 mm\). Note that you don't calculate the
surface area of the test tube, which would be close to a cylinder. Only
half of that area is irradiated by the sun. And this 50\% area is not
evenly illuminated, since the angle to the sun is changing around the
cylinder. The effective illuminated area can be seen in the shadow -
nearly a rectangle.

From the measured intensity we can calculate the intensity on the
surface of the sun. The two needed parameters are the distance from
earth to sun \(1 AU = 1.495978707\times 10^{11} m\) and the average
radius of the sun \(r_{sun}=6.955\times 10^8 m\). The intensity is
inversly proportional to the square of the distances, given by the
intensity formula above:

\[P=I_{sun}A_{sun}=I_{earth}A_{AU}\]

Since the area \(A\) is calculated by \(A=4\pi r^2\) the factor \(4\pi\)
cancels out and we get

\[I_{sun}=I_{earth}\frac{r_{AU}^2}{r_{sun}^2}\]

    In our final step we rearange the Stefan-Boltzmann law and bring the
area on the left side of the equation, which gives us the intensity at
suns surface.

\[\frac{P}{A_{sun}}=I_{sun}=\sigma T^4\]

The temperature can now be calculated using

\[T = \sqrt[\uproot{3}4]{\frac{I_{sun}}{\sigma}}\]
